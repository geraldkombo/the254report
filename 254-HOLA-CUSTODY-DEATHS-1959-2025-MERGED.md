# An Uncatalogued Manuscript at the Kenya National Archives Reveals What Really Happened at Hola Detention Camp in 1959

**A former detainee's uncatalogued manuscript at the Kenya National Archives provides the only known eyewitness account of the Hola massacre and reveals the same pattern of official lies and impunity connecting 1959 to a 2025 police cell killing.**

*By **Gerald Kombo**, The 254 Report | June 2026*

## How This Investigation Was Conducted

This investigation draws on four months of archival research at the Kenya National Archives in Nairobi, where I identified an uncatalogued manuscript titled "Maumau: An Account of the Darkest Days of the Emergency" by Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie, a former detainee who served as the Kapenguria trial defence coordinator and later as a Central Committee and War Council member. The manuscript, accession record not publicly indexed, was located through cross-referencing detainee names against camp records. It runs more than 370 handwritten pages and has not been previously cited in journalism or published scholarship.

Additional sources include direct reporting from the Wakati Wetu Festival on October 22, 2025 where Hon. Paul Muite discussed the three betrayals; the Hansard record of the British House of Commons debates on Hola (June 16 and July 27, 1959); the 2013 London High Court settlement documents for the Mau Mau compensation case; the Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) investigation files for the Albert Ojwang case; the Kibera High Court bail ruling of June 2025; and the Kenya National Archives' own published finding aid for the Mau Mau collection.

## The Manuscript Discovery: An Original Investigative Technique

**How Did an Uncatalogued Manuscript Become the Key to Understanding Kenya's Custody Death Pattern?**

Standard histories of the Mau Mau period rely on colonial archives, oral histories collected decades after the events, and Caroline Elkins's 2005 study "Imperial Reckoning: The Untold Story of Britain's Gulag in Kenya." The Wanjie manuscript is different. It was written by a participant who was present at both the Kapenguria trial and the Hola massacre, it was composed while the events were still fresh, and it was deposited at the Kenya National Archives but never catalogued for public access. Its existence came to light through a systematic cross-reference of detainee names against the archive's unpublished holdings.

**What Makes the Wanjie Manuscript Uniquely Valuable?**

Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie was not a passive observer. He was assigned by his committee to coordinate defence logistics for the Kapenguria trial from November 1952 to March 1953. "This assignment had been approved by our leaders," he writes. "They needed me in case they wanted to have some facts clarified or wanted anything for their defence."

He managed international lawyers, including Mr Davies from Nigeria and Mr Chamalal from India, who joined the defence team led by British barrister D.N. Pritt. "Several lawyers, and particularly from India, were refused to participate in the case," Wanjie records. "Two of them were refused to leave their aeroplanes at the risk of being arrested and charged with wrongful entry into the country."

When the movement escalated from political organizing to armed struggle, Wanjie became a War Council member for Murang'a district and participated in the Nairobi underground cell structure. His manuscript lists the full War Council membership by district, the forest generals including Dedan Kimathi and Stanley Mathenge, the eleven operational divisions within Nairobi city, and the oath administration hierarchy. No other single document in the Kenya National Archives or any published source assembles this information in one place from a firsthand participant.

## What the Manuscript Reveals About the Hola Massacre

**What Happened on March 3, 1959, at Hola Detention Camp?**

Wanjie was a sick detainee in the dispensary group on the morning of March 3, 1959. He describes the division of detainees into three groups. "One group which consisted of sick detainees, which included myself, we taken to the camp dispensary," he writes. "The other group which was not sick but looked physically weak was detailed to clean the camp. The third group, which consisted of 85 hard-core young detainees was the one which was to be used as guinea pigs for resuming work."

The 85 detainees were led outside, each guarded by five warders armed with rifles, sticks, and batons. "It was about 8.0 a.m. in the morning when each group was ordered to its assignment," Wanjie writes. "It was only minutes when shocking screams and lamentations defeated our ears from the group which was taken outside to work."

Those in the dispensary were forced to squat with heads down. "If one raised his head to see what was happening, he was hit very hard on the head by the warders who guarded us. The screaming became more and more intensive."

**How Did the Red Cross Doctor Respond to the Killings?**

The first bodies arrived before midday. "It was then I saw two of the 85 detainees being brought inside the camp, already dead," Wanjie writes. "Then another lot of three, all dead. The beating continued and continued until mid-day."

A Red Cross doctor was present in the dispensary. "The Doctor was so afflicted that he burst into tears and explained that it was not his job to treat corpses," Wanjie records.

Wanjie names neither the doctor nor the Red Cross nurse, noting only "Miss..." for the nurse. Both, he says, risked their positions to disclose that the detainees had died of beating. "If it was not for the two Red Cross workers, we would not have survived to tell this story. We shall never forget them. They had risked their own positions and personal prestige because of us."

**How Did the Colonial Government Cover Up the Massacre?**

Kenya officials, including the Governor, Sir Evelyn Baring, told the world that the eleven people had died after drinking poisoned water from a cart. Wanjie writes: "I don't know what historians would say of this. I am not one of them."

The eleven men who died at Hola on March 3, 1959, were: Kabui Kaman, Ndungu Kibaki, Mwema Kinuthia, Kinyanjui Njoroge, Koroma Mburu, Karanja Munuthi, Ikeno Ikiro, Migwi Ndegwa, Kaman Karanja, Mungai Githi, and Ngugi Karitie. Their names are confirmed by the July 2020 Nation Africa monument inscription at the site and match Wanjie's account.

After the massacre, Wanjie and other survivors were refused food for 19 days. The Red Cross doctor kept them alive with a liquid he described as ordinary water. "He told us to obey him in that respect because he knew what he was doing," Wanjie writes. "The merciless colonial beasts at the camp asked him why he was bothering about us. He told them that he was only giving us water and that he was merely doing his job as a doctor for which he was paid by the government."

When Sir Evelyn Baring visited the camp, Wanjie confronted him. Baring replied, as Wanjie records: "You are the worst Mau Mau and no one should have mercy upon you. You have lived with hard heads and have refused to co-operate with the government. Therefore, you will never be allowed to return to your Kikuyu country and you will perish right in here." Then Baring added: "Even if I found you dead, I could not have been worried a bit."

No senior colonial official lost their position over Hola. The camp was later removed from maps and renamed Galole. In 1971, President Jomo Kenyatta ordered the name restored to Hola, an attempt to reclaim the site's memory that sits in tension with his government's simultaneous suppression of Mau Mau survivors. No compensation reached the families. No warders faced murder charges.

## The Kapenguria Trial and the Movement's Organizational Structure

**How Did the Freedom Movement Organize Its Defence at Kapenguria?**

The Kapenguria trial of Jomo Kenyatta and five other leaders lasted 58 days from November 1952 to March 1953. Wanjie's committee assigned him to coordinate defence logistics. He managed witness accommodation, transportation, and communication between the accused and the legal team. "Every morning of the trial, our lawyers gave me a lift in their car while our witnesses were transported by Robinson Mwangi Njagi in his car," he writes.

The defence team included D.N. Pritt from Britain, Mr Davies from Nigeria, Mr Chamalal from India, and local lawyers including Mr D'Souza, Jaswant Singh, A.R. Kapila, Mr Thomson of Tanzania, and Argwings-Kodhek. Wanjie notes that two lawyers from India were refused entry to the country.

Wanjie was barred from the courtroom. "It is nevertheless very sad to record that despite our enthusiasm, we were not allowed even on one single day to enter the court room to witness how the case was being conducted," he writes. Instead, he communicated with the accused using a coded Kikuyu exchange as they were escorted past. "We only chanced to see them at a distance as they were being escorted in and out of the court, and facing the opposite direction, I would ask them: Kuri o kuo? (Are you still alive/there?) One of them would ask us: Kuri noogo (Is smoke still there?) This way, unless one was a Kikuyu, the askaris would not know what was passing between us."

One prosecution witness, a woman named Peninah Wambui, defied the colonial court. Wanjie writes: "She refused to tell lies and said in court that she knew nothing about any of the accused. As a hostile witness, she was remanded together with the accused. After the trial, she was released on condition that she maintained absolute silence over the Kapenguria affair. She was handed over to me by Mzee Kenyatta, who instructed me to take care of her. I came with her to Nairobi where I rented a room for her and maintained her until when I was arrested and detained."

**What Did the Movement's War Council and Nairobi Cells Look Like?**

When the Central Committee became a War Council, Wanjie records the full membership by district: Murang'a, Nyeri, Kiambu, Embu, Meru, Machakos, and Rift Valley Province. The forest generals included Field Marshals Dedan Kimathi Waciuri and Stanley Mathenge Mirugi, Generals China (Jarukiu Itote), Mbaria Kaniu, and Kago Eboko, and at least 20 other named commanders.

The Nairobi structure operated across eleven divisions: Bahati, Kirimaini, Kileleshwa, Pumwani, Gatharaini, Shauri Moyo, Makadara, Kariakor, Ngara, Panani, Mathaiga, and Kahawa. Each division had its own brigadier, major, R.S.M., and supporting officers. "Our Nairobi organisation was very effective because we had access to almost every part of the city," Wanjie writes. "All these people were by appearance, very ordinary people and you would not have distinguished them from the rest under normal circumstances."

This organizational detail, recorded by a participant who held positions in both the political and armed wings, challenges the colonial portrayal of a chaotic cult and provides a primary-source reference for historians and journalists.

## The Three Betrayals: How the Pattern Persisted Across Seven Decades

**Why Did Britain's Official Response Protect Senior Officials After Hola?**

The Hansard record of the British House of Commons shows Colonial Secretary Lennox-Boyd defending the Hola response on June 16 and July 27, 1959. He described the detainees as "hard core men, some of them certainly with records of violence of a most repellent kind" and framed the beatings as an unfortunate consequence of a rehabilitation program. No minister resigned. No compensation was offered.

The cover-up extended beyond the immediate official lie. Historian Caroline Elkins documents that Operation Legacy, a British colonial program, involved the systematic destruction of documents relating to the Emergency before independence in 1963. The state did not merely lie about Hola. It attempted to erase the evidence that would allow future accountability.

**What Did the Crown on the Cap Badge Reveal About Responsibility During the 2013 London Trial?**

During the 2013 London trial, British lawyers argued that Kenya's own people committed the beatings, not Her Majesty's government. Wambugu Wa Nyingi, a Mau Mau veteran who survived sixteen colonial detention camps, asked to pose a question: did the Queen's Counsel know that the homeguards wore a kofia bearing the Kenya Police cap badge with the British Crown insignia? The question was never answered directly. Britain settled out of court for £19.9 million for 5,228 living Mau Mau victims, plus an official apology read in the House of Commons.

But the settlement had limits. Britain admitted no legal liability. Compensation was calculated per victim, not per atrocity. And the verification process of who qualified as a genuine Mau Mau veteran became a new source of controversy.

**Why Did Kenya's Post-Independence Government Keep Mau Mau Criminalized?**

Hon. Paul Muite, the Kenyan lawyer who led the 2013 case, identifies this as the third betrayal. In 1968, five years after independence, President Jomo Kenyatta's government re-gazetted Mau Mau as a terrorist organization under the Societies Act. The same people who had fought for independence were legally classified as criminals by the government they had helped install.

The gazette notice remained in force for thirty-five years until President Mwai Kibaki repealed it in 2003. For nearly four decades, the freedom movement in Kenya was legally indistinguishable from a terrorist group. As Wambugu Wa Nyingi explained in the London trial, a terrorist organization cannot file a lawsuit. The 1968 gazette was not symbolic. It was a legal barrier that protected Britain from accountability for three decades.

## The Modern Recurrence: Albert Ojwang, 2025

**What Happened to Albert Omondi Ojwang at Central Police Station?**

On June 6, 2025, Albert Omondi Ojwang, a 31-year-old teacher and blogger from Homa Bay County, was arrested at his home after a complaint from Eliud Lagat, the Deputy Inspector General of Police, who accused him of spreading false statements on social media. Police transported Albert more than 400 kilometers to Central Police Station on Harry Thuku Road in Nairobi, booking him late that night. By morning he was dead.

The first police statement claimed he had hit his head against the cell wall repeatedly, an apparent suicide. Government pathologists recorded severe blunt-force head injuries, neck compression, and multiple bruises across his body, findings consistent with assault. Faith Odhiambo, president of the Law Society of Kenya, said the autopsy report clearly showed Ojwang had been tortured and brutally murdered. The Inspector General later withdrew the suicide claim and apologized for releasing false information. The Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) found that cameras monitoring Albert's cell were switched off or contained unexplained gaps.

**Why Did Accountability Stop Below the Senior Commander?**

On June 23, 2025, the Director of Public Prosecutions approved murder charges against six people: Samson Kiprotich Talaam (Officer Commanding Central Police Station), James Mukhwana (day cell sentry), Peter Kimani (night cell sentry), John Ginge Gitau (a police officer under investigation for a separate murder case), Gin Ammitou Abwao, and Brian Mwaniki Njue. All six pleaded not guilty. The Kibera High Court denied them bail.

The Deputy Inspector General whose complaint triggered the arrest was not charged. Nine days after the killing, Lagat issued a statement on official Kenya Police Service letterhead announcing he would step aside to allow investigations. Months later, after investigators indicated he would not face charges, he returned to his post.

The sequence matched Hola: a death in custody, an official explanation that collapsed, and accountability that flowed downward, not upward.

**How Did Digital-Age Protesters Connect to the Mau Mau Legacy?**

News of Albert's death turned online outrage into street protest. Young Kenyans organized demonstrations via social media in Nairobi, Mombasa, Homa Bay, and other towns. Protesters marched with banners reading "Justice for Albert Ojwang" on Kimathi Street in downtown Nairobi, named after Field Marshal Dedan Kimathi Waciuri, a commander in the freedom movement who was captured, tried, and hanged by colonial authorities in 1957. Wanjie's manuscript records that Kimathi was one of two field marshals leading the forest fighters, alongside Stanley Mathenge, and that military titles were adopted by forest leaders to command respect and maintain discipline. The protesters staged a die-in on central Nairobi roads and carried the national flag between banks and office towers. By December 2025, the protests had faded from national headlines even as the court case dragged on, echoing how Hola was buried under official lies and bureaucratic indifference.

## What This Investigation Proves

**What Links Hola 1959, the London Case, and Central Police Station 2025?**

The continuity is not in the specific violence or the political context. It is in the state's response to harm committed under its authority. At Hola, the colonial government lied and protected senior officials. In the 2013 London case, the British government compensated victims but admitted no liability, leaving the question of who qualified as a genuine victim unresolved. At Central Police Station, the police lied, the senior commander stepped aside temporarily, and junior officers face murder charges alone while the man whose complaint triggered the arrest returned to his post.

Muite's three betrayals explain why this pattern persists. The first established that the state could take land and liberty without accountability. The second established that detention violence would be met with official lies. The third established that even after independence, the new government would not break the pattern. It would suppress the same people, deny their claims, and protect the institutional structures that enabled the violence.

Each case produced independent evidence that refuted the official version. In Wanjie's manuscript, the Hola massacre is described by a man who heard the screams from the dispensary, saw the bodies carried in, and watched the Red Cross doctor break down. In the London courtroom, Wambugu Wa Nyingi asked a question about a cap badge that Britain settled rather than answer. At Central Police Station, the cameras were switched off, and the pathologist's findings contradicted the police statement.

The unanswered question is whether Kenya can build institutions that break this cycle. Wanjie's manuscript, Muite's case files, and Albert Ojwang's post-mortem report all demand the same answer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What was the Hola detention camp massacre?**

On March 3, 1959, British colonial warders beat eleven detainees to death at Hola detention camp in what is now Tana River County, Kenya. The government initially blamed poisoned water, but medical evidence proved the men died from beatings. An uncatalogued manuscript by Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie, a detainee present in the camp dispensary that morning, provides the only known firsthand eyewitness account. No senior officials lost their positions.

**Who was Hon. Paul Muite and what did his 2013 case achieve?**

Hon. Paul Muite is a Kenyan lawyer who led the legal team that won £19.9 million in compensation from the British government for 5,228 Mau Mau victims, plus an official apology. His case established that former colonial subjects could sue Britain for colonial atrocities. The case took fifty-eight years to bring because Kenya's own government had classified Mau Mau as a terrorist organization until 2003.

**Why did Kenya's post-independence government classify Mau Mau as terrorists?**

In 1968, five years after independence, President Jomo Kenyatta's government re-gazetted Mau Mau as a terrorist organization under the Societies Act. The classification remained until 2003 under President Mwai Kibaki. This prevented survivors from organizing legally or filing compensation claims for thirty-five years.

**Who was Albert Omondi Ojwang?**

Albert Omondi Ojwang was a 31-year-old teacher and blogger from Homa Bay County, Kenya. He was arrested on June 6, 2025, on a complaint from the Deputy Inspector General of Police and died in a cell at Central Police Station, Nairobi. Government pathologists found injuries consistent with assault, contradicting the initial police claim of suicide. Six people have been charged and denied bail.

**Has Britain ever been held accountable for colonial atrocities in Kenya?**

Britain paid £19.9 million in 2013 to 5,228 Mau Mau victims but admitted no legal liability. The settlement did not require Britain to verify each claimant individually against colonial detention records, leaving the question of who qualified as a genuine victim unresolved.

**Who was Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie and why does his manuscript matter?**

Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie was a Kapenguria trial defence coordinator, a Central Committee and War Council member for Murang'a district, and a detainee present at Hola on March 3, 1959. His manuscript, held but uncatalogued at the Kenya National Archives, is the only known firsthand account that covers the Kapenguria trial logistics, the War Council and Nairobi cell organization, the oath administration hierarchy, and the Hola massacre eyewitness narrative. It has not been previously cited in journalism or published scholarship.
