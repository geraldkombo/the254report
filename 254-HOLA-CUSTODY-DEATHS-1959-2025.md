# Kenya Custody Deaths: Hola Detention 1959 to Central Police Station 2025

**A former detainee's handwritten manuscript and a blogger's killing in a Nairobi cell reveal the same state pattern across six decades: official lies that collapse under independent scrutiny, followed by accountability that stops at junior ranks.**

> In March 1959, eleven detainees were beaten to death at Hola detention camp in colonial Kenya. The government blamed poisoned water. In June 2025, blogger Albert Omondi Ojwang died in a cell at Central Police Station, Nairobi. Police claimed suicide. Pathologists ruled assault. Between these two deaths lies independence, a new constitution, and the same official pattern: an implausible story, evidence that demolishes it, and no senior held responsible.

## The Human Truth

Kenya's custody deaths are not a series of isolated failures. They are a structural pattern of state violence that has survived independence, constitutional reform, and generational turnover. At its center is a dispute over what happens when someone dies in state custody: the government offers an explanation that strains credibility, independent investigators produce evidence that refutes it, and accountability terminates at the lowest possible rank. This pattern connects two events sixty-six years apart. In 1959, Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie watched eleven men die under British warders' clubs at Hola, a remote detention camp on the Tana River. In 2025, Albert Omondi Ojwang, a teacher and blogger from Homa Bay County, entered Central Police Station alive and left as a corpse between two police statement revisions.

## The Movement That Named Itself

**How Did the Freedom Movement in Kenya Organize Before Armed Struggle?**

The movement that the colonial government and settlers called Mau Mau was a disciplined political organization governed by a Central Committee, a secret supreme body called Muhimu, and a War Council that coordinated operations once armed struggle began. Wanjie's unpublished manuscript, titled "Maumau: An Account of the Darkest Days of the Emergency" and held at the Kenya National Archives in Nairobi, documents these structures in detail across more than 370 handwritten pages. The movement's motto in Kikuyu was Kuuga na Gwika, meaning say and act, a rejection of leaders who made promises but took no risks. It collected membership fees from supporters, administered binding oaths that established loyalty and secrecy, and maintained an internal justice system: anyone accused of wrongdoing away from home had to be returned to their own area for judgment by people who knew them.

**What Does Wanjie's Manuscript Reveal About the Kapenguria Trial?**

Wanjie's account includes logistics that standard histories omit. While the Kapenguria Six, including Jomo Kenyatta, faced trial from 1952 to 1953 in the remote Rift Valley town of Kapenguria, the movement organized legal defence coordination, witness protection, and communication lines between the accused and the outside. The Central Committee at Kiburi House in Nairobi functioned as a war room, directing strategy for both the political and armed wings. This organizational sophistication contradicts the colonial portrayal of a chaotic cult and explains why the crackdown took years and thousands of arrests to suppress. Military titles such as general and field marshal, Wanjie records, were chosen locally by commanders who needed authority to enforce discipline in harsh forest conditions.

**How Did the Detention Camp System Operate?**

The colonial detention network was not a single facility but a chain of camps designed to hold, classify, and "rehabilitate" suspected movement members through forced labour and psychological pressure. Wanjie's own detention journey moved through Nairobi West, Athi River, Mackinnon Road, Manyani, and Sayusi Island before arriving at Hola, where hard-core detainees classified as "the blackest of the black" faced the worst conditions. Each camp had a specific function: screening at Nairobi West, classification at Athi River, labour at Mackinnon Road. Hola was the end of the line for those who refused to cooperate. This systematic infrastructure mirrors the architecture of modern state detention, where suspects are moved between police stations, court cells, and prison facilities in a process that makes accountability harder to trace.

## What Happened at Hola

**What Happened on March 3, 1959, at Hola Detention Camp?**

Guards divided detainees into three groups: one sent to the kitchen, one including Wanjie to the dispensary, and a third younger group to forced field labour digging irrigation furrows with hoes, basins, and spades. From the dispensary, Wanjie heard what he describes as the most agonizing screams of his detention. Warders beat the labour group in clusters of five, whipping them until at least eleven men lay dead. Their names: Kabui Kaman, Ndungu Kibaki, Mwema Kinuthia, Kinyanjui Njoroge, Koroma Mburu, Karanja Munuthi, Ikeno Ikiro, Migwi Ndegwa, Kaman Karanja, Mungai Githi, and Ngugi Karitie. A young Red Cross doctor broke down in tears as bodies were carried into the dispensary. The colonial government announced the men had died after drinking poisoned or contaminated water, a story repeated to Parliament in London until medical evidence and outside investigations made the lie unsustainable.

**How Did the State Try to Conceal the Massacre?**

Even after acknowledging the beatings caused the deaths, no senior colonial official lost their position. The camp was later removed from maps and renamed Galole, an erasure Wanjie interprets as a second attempt to bury its history. No compensation reached the families. No warders faced murder charges. The message was clear: the state could kill in detention, lie about it, and absorb the cost of exposure. This pattern would repeat in independent Kenya.

## Albert Ojwang

**What Happened to Albert Omondi Ojwang at Central Police Station?**

On the night of June 7, 2025, Albert Omondi Ojwang, a teacher and blogger from Homa Bay County, was arrested at his home after a complaint from Eliud Lagat, the Deputy Inspector General of Police, who accused him of spreading false statements on social media. Police transported Albert more than 350 kilometers to Central Police Station on Harry Thuku Road in Nairobi, booking him late that night. By morning he was dead. The first police statement claimed he had hit his head against the cell wall repeatedly, an apparent suicide. Government pathologists recorded severe blunt-force head injuries, neck compression, and multiple bruises across his body, findings consistent with assault, not self-harm. The Inspector General later withdrew the suicide claim and apologized for releasing false information. The Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) found that cameras monitoring Albert's cell were switched off or contained unexplained gaps.

**Why Did Accountability Stop Below the Senior Commander?**

Prosecutors charged six people with murder: three police officers and three civilians or fellow inmates. All pleaded not guilty. The Deputy Inspector General whose complaint triggered the arrest was not charged. Nine days after the killing, Lagat issued a statement on official Kenya Police Service letterhead announcing he would step aside to allow investigations. Months later, after investigators indicated he would not face charges, he returned to his post. The sequence matched Hola: a death in custody, an official explanation that collapsed, and accountability that flowed downward, not upward.

**How Did Digital-Age Protesters Respond?**

News of Albert's death turned online outrage into street protest. Young Kenyans organized demonstrations via social media in Nairobi, Mombasa, Homa Bay, and other towns. Protesters marched with banners reading "Justice for Albert Ojwang" and staged a die-in on central Nairobi roads, lying across the tarmac while others raised posters with Albert's portrait. They carried the national flag between banks and office towers. At Albert's funeral, mourners marched toward a nearby police station, chanting against deaths in custody. By December 2025, the protests faded from national headlines even as the court case dragged on, showing the same trajectory as Hola: an event that produces momentary public fury before settling into bureaucratic quiet.

## The Connection

**What Links Hola 1959 to Central Police Station 2025 Across Sixty-Six Years?**

The continuity is not in the specific violence or the political context. It is in the state's response to a death that occurs on its watch. In both cases, the first official explanation was demonstrably false. In both cases, independent evidence exposed the lie. In both cases, no senior official faced consequences. Wanjie's manuscript preserves the account that colonial authorities tried to erase with a poisoned water story and a name change. The post-mortem reports, IPOA findings, court files, and protest photographs around Albert's death perform the same function for independent Kenya. The digitally organized Gen Z protesters may never read Wanjie's manuscript, but their demand echoes its central claim: the state must account for what happens inside its walls.

**What Question Remains Unanswered?**

Existing coverage of both cases focuses on the immediate facts: who died, how, and whether anyone was punished. It does not ask why the institutional pattern persists across fundamentally different governments and legal frameworks. The colonial administration of 1959 and the democratically elected government of 2025 share a common response to custody deaths. Neither has built a system in which a senior official is held accountable when a person dies in state custody. This question, the structural continuity of impunity, is the gap that Wanjie's manuscript and Albert's case file together expose.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What was the Hola detention camp massacre?**

On March 3, 1959, British colonial warders beat eleven detainees to death at Hola detention camp in what is now Tana River County, Kenya. The government initially blamed poisoned water, but medical evidence proved the men died from beatings. No senior officials lost their positions. The camp was later removed from maps and renamed Galole.

**Who was Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie?**

Eliud Mutonyi Wanjie was a former detainee and organizer in the freedom movement in Kenya who survived detention at Nairobi West, Athi River, Mackinnon Road, Manyani, Sayusi Island, and Hola. His handwritten manuscript, "Maumau: An Account of the Darkest Days of the Emergency," runs more than 370 pages and is held at the Kenya National Archives in Nairobi.

**Who was Albert Omondi Ojwang?**

Albert Omondi Ojwang was a teacher and blogger from Homa Bay County, Kenya. He was arrested on June 7, 2025, on a complaint from the Deputy Inspector General of Police and died in a cell at Central Police Station, Nairobi, the same night. Government pathologists found injuries consistent with assault, contradicting the initial police claim of suicide.

**Why do Kenya's custody deaths show a recurring pattern?**

Both the colonial government's response to Hola and the Kenya Police Service's response to Albert's death followed an identical sequence: an implausible official explanation, independent evidence that refuted it, and accountability that stopped at junior ranks. The pattern has survived independence (1963) and the promulgation of a new constitution (2010).

**How many people die in police custody in Kenya each year?**

The Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) records multiple custody death cases annually, but the total number is disputed because police stations control the initial reporting. IPOA investigations frequently find that official accounts differ from post-mortem and forensic evidence, as in Albert's case.
